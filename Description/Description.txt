The Global Climate Change Summary :
It provides a multi-dimensional view of temperature data from the mid-18th century through the early 21st century, focusing heavily on regional trends in South Asia and the Middle East.
1. Historical Temperature Trends (1753–2028)
The Global Temperature Trend line graph shows a steady, volatile increase in average temperatures starting around the 1800s.
Major Anomaly: There is a massive, unexplained spike in the data around the year 2003, where the "Average Temperature" values briefly jump to nearly 200K before returning to a baseline of approximately 100K by 2028. This suggests a significant data outlier or a specific localized event within the dataset.
2. Geographical Temperature Distribution
The dashboard highlights a clear correlation between latitude and heat:
Latitude Correlation: The Temperature vs. Latitude scatter plot confirms that temperatures are highest near the equator (low latitudes) and steadily decrease as one moves toward the North Pole.
Regional Hotspots: The Temperature by Country map shows a high density of data points across India, the Middle East (Abadan, Abu Dhabi), and East Africa (Asmara). The concentration of orange markers in South Asia indicates that this region represents a large portion of the dataset.
3. Top Warming Cities
The bar chart identifies specific urban areas with the highest average temperatures:
Leading Cities: Avadi, Ambattur, and Alandur are the top three cities listed, followed by Alama and Bobo Dioulasso.
Data Note: While the x-axis scale for this chart is labeled 0–30, the data labels for the top cities are significantly higher (e.g., 63,060), indicating a potential unit mismatch or a specific weighted index used for "warming" rather than raw.
4. Summary Insights
Data Density: The dashboard is heavily weighted toward cities in the Indian subcontinent, with many of the "Top Warming Cities" (like Bellary and Adoni) located in this region.
Missing Data: The map indicates 71 unknown locations, suggesting that a portion of the recorded temperature data could not be precisely mapped to a specific city or country.
